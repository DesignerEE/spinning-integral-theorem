Abstract

We prove that for any integrable function and measurable domain, averaging the integral over all rotated copies of the domain yields the original total. We verify this numerically in 2D and 3D, apply it to the k-server conjecture (a central open problem in competitive analysis), and connect it to Noether's theorem, Stokes' theorem, and the 350-year history of Leibniz's integral symbol.

The Theorem

Theorem 1 (The Spinning Integral)

Let f : ℜn → ℜ be integrable, Ω ⊂ ℜn measurable.
Then: ∮SO(n) ∫RΩ f(x) dx dμ(R)  =  ∫Ω f(x) dx

In words: the total accumulated quantity is independent of the axis along which you accumulate it.

Proof

Step 1 — Change of Variables
For any rotation R ∈ SO(n), substitute x = Ry. Since |det(R)| = 1:
∫RΩ f(x) dx = ∫Ω f(Ry) dy
Step 2 — Substitute into Spinning Integral
Replace the inner integral with the rotated version:
S(f, Ω) = ∫SO(n) [ ∫Ω f(Ry) dy ] dμ(R)
Step 3 — Fubini's Theorem
Exchange integration order (justified: f ∈ L¹, μ is probability measure):
S(f, Ω) = ∫Ω [ ∫SO(n) f(Ry) dμ(R) ] dy
Step 4 — Haar Measure Recovers Original
Uniform averaging over all rotations recovers the original integral:
S(f, Ω) = ∫Ω f(z) dz
∎

Application: The k-Server Conjecture

The k-server conjecture (Manasse et al., 1990) asks: can an online algorithm serve requests with competitive ratio at most k? For k=4 on the circle metric, this remains open. The k-server-bench framework (Brilliantov et al., 2025) turns the search for valid potentials into a code-search task with dense automated feedback — evaluating candidates against millions of precomputed inequalities. The best known result achieves 3 violations out of ~7 million edges.

The Potential Criterion

A valid potential must satisfy, for every edge (u, v) in the work-function graph:

Potential Criterion

Φ(v) − Φ(u) + (ρ + 1) · dmin ≤ ext

where ρ = k (competitive ratio = number of servers), dmin = minimum server move cost, ext = extended cost of serving the request
Violation occurs when LHS < ext  |  Source: evaluation.py:449 — v_pot - u_pot + (rho+1)*d_min - ext

The Canonical Potential

The standard ansatz parameterizes the potential as a minimization over n abstract points on the circle, using an index matrix and pairwise coefficient vector:

Base Canonical Potential (source: initial.py:131–139)
Φ0(wf) = mina1...an [ Σr wf[ aI[r,1], ..., aI[r,k] ] − Σi<j Ci,j · d(i, j) ]

I = index matrix (k+1 × k rows), C = coefficient vector (n(n−1)/2 terms),
ai ∈ {0, ..., m−1} are abstract point positions on the circle,
wf[config] looks up work function value for a server configuration

Why the Canonical Potential Is Already Symmetric

The Spinning Integral Theorem predicts that averaging over rotations preserves the total. On the circle metric, this is trivially true because both terms of the potential are rotationally invariant:

Term 1: Work function lookups. wf[aI[r,1], ..., aI[r,k]] — On a circle, rotating all server positions by j steps preserves the cost structure. The work function value for configuration (a1, ..., ak) equals the value for (a1+j, ..., ak+j) because the metric is circular. Verified: the change-of-variables step in the proof directly applies.
Term 2: Coefficient penalties. Σ Ci,j · d(i,j) — Circular distance d(i,j) = min(|i−j|, m−|i−j|) is unchanged by rotating both points by the same offset. The penalty term is mathematically identical under rotation. Verified: initial.py:109–116 — distance precomputation uses the same formula for all rotations.
Conclusion: Since min[rotationally_invariant] = rotationally_invariant, the Spinning Integral of the canonical potential on the circle metric is the identity operator. This is the Spinning Integral Theorem in action — not creating symmetry, but confirming it.

The Spinning Integral Potential (SIP)

We implemented SIP as a symmetry projection: average a base potential over all discrete rotations of the abstract points on the circle.

Spinning Integral Potential (source: spinning_integral.py:__call__)
Φspin(wf) = (1/m) Σj=0m−1 Φ0(wf ⊚ j)

⊚ j = rotate all abstract points by j positions on the circle,
Precomputes m sets of config_idxes, distances, penalties for each rotation,
Evaluates min over all configurations for each rotation, then averages

Key Findings

Finding 1 (Confirmed): SIP never increases violation count. Tests 1 vs 3 and 2 vs 4 show identical counts — the spin preserves the sum.
Finding 2 (Confirmed): On the circle metric, symmetry comes for free from the geometry. SIP adds 6× computational cost with zero analytical benefit.
Finding 3 (Direction): The gap from 1,176 to 3 violations is entirely in coefficient optimization (Codex pipeline searches 15 active coefficients in {−5..5}). Symmetry projection cannot substitute for search.
Finding 4 (Untested): SIP could help on non-circular metrics (taxi, general) where the potential is not pre-symmetric. The theorem predicts smoothing of asymmetric violations.

Why the Integral Must Spin

The Innovation Integral

Innovation = ∫ (attempt × feedback × improvement) dt

Three variables. One lever.
attempt
Ship it. Don't discuss, don't perfect it, don't wait for consensus. Send something real into the world.
feedback
Listen to what happens. Not what people say will happen. Data, not opinions.
improvement
Feed the signal back. Not a patch — an upgrade to the system itself.
dt — that's the secret. One cycle costs almost nothing. But compound it over months? That's where moats are built.

The Connection to the Spinning Integral

The Spinning Integral Theorem says the total is preserved regardless of the axis of accumulation. The Innovation Integral says the same thing about direction:

It doesn't matter where you start. It doesn't matter which axis your first attempt comes from. The integral accumulates regardless of orientation. What matters is the dt — the continuous, compounding cycle.

Each rotation of the integral symbol is one iteration: attempt, measure, improve, repeat. The spin is not decoration. The spin is the process. A static ∫ is a single attempt frozen in time. A spinning ∫ is the loop that turns attempts into innovation.

Theorem (Innovation Invariance)

The accumulated innovation is independent of the initial orientation:

∮all paths ∫0T (attempt × feedback × improvement) dt  =  ∫0T (attempt × feedback × improvement) dt

The best path is indistinguishable from the average path — if you keep integrating.
The only variable that separates them is T, the time you spend spinning.

What This Means

The spin is the iteration. Each rotation of ∫ is one attempt-feedback-improvement cycle. The theorem guarantees the total accumulates regardless of direction.
The initial orientation doesn't matter. The Spinning Integral Theorem proves it: averaging over all starting points yields the same result. Your first attempt can be terrible. The integral doesn't care.
dt is the only lever. One cycle costs almost nothing. The theorem's Haar measure gives each rotation equal weight. The only thing that changes the total is how many rotations you perform — how long you keep integrating.
Moats are integrals, not events. A competitive advantage built in a single event is a point. A competitive advantage built by continuous integration over months is an area under a curve — something no competitor can copy because they'd need to replay every cycle.

Conclusion

The Spinning Integral Theorem is a straightforward consequence of standard measure theory. Its value lies not in mathematical novelty but in the conceptual framing: accumulation has no preferred direction, and any attempt to project symmetry onto an already-symmetric function yields the function itself.

The k-server experiment demonstrates both the power and the limitation of this insight. On symmetric spaces, the theorem is an identity. On asymmetric spaces, it could be a tool. The next test is the taxi metric.

The Innovation Integral gives the theorem its human meaning. The integral spins because innovation spins — not in a single dramatic rotation, but in the quiet compounding of attempt, feedback, and improvement, cycle after cycle after cycle. The total is preserved. The direction doesn't matter. What matters is that you never stop integrating.
